04 July 2026

Wife went to the Midwest for a week - wedding and family/friend visits.

I took off the week from work. Did some chores. Got some riding in. Saw the doc.

Doc said I should start going for some walks now and then, so, on Tuesday the 23rd, I grabbed the motorcycle, packed a pair of sneakers, and rode up to Tillamook State Forest to find a nice little trail to walk.

Perhaps, hindsight being 20/20 . . . which, frankly, I kind of doubt? I mean . . . really? Do we think we really know enough about our past actions that we know, with perfect clarity, how things would have turned out?

And yet . . .

To ride the motorcycle an hour, one way, to walk a trail I've never walked on before while my wife is 2000 miles away on International Widows' Day?

So perhaps, hindsight being 20/20 . . . ish . . . I should - possibly? - have figured that things wouldn't go smoothly.

Had a nice little ride. Found a nice little trail head. Put on the sneakers. Made it about one hundred meters down the trail and down I went.

Like, down down.

Hard on my left knee.

Like, hard hard.

Tried getting on my back and felt my left ankle go.

But not, like, go go.

Had a bit of a laugh. Sat for a moment. Got up. Hobbled back to the bike.

The bike (PDF) was on its side.

Honda motorcycles are notorious for their big lean while on the side stand and while it was nearing a hundred degrees in Willamette Valley that meant heavy fog on the coast and a marine layer just off the coast . . . all this is to say that it was wet on the coast and I had parked in mud.

Slippy slidey don't park your ridey on it kinda mud.

After another good laugh, and just as I was getting ready to pick up the bike, I realized I had a decision to make. Head to the local ER and maybe, if I'd broken something, not be able to get home on my bike and not be able to call wife to come pick me up . . . or . . . ride home, see how the knee and ankle were doing, and if I then felt I had to I could go to the ER near home in something with four wheels.

Traded sneakers for motorcycle boots. Picked up the bike. Rode an hour home. Changed into pants I could easily lift over my injuries to show the nice medical professionals - it hurts here and here, doc - when the time came. Switched to the car. Went to the ER.

By the time I got to the ER I could no longer bend my knee, so I parked, Edgar-suit walked (YouTube) my way into the ER, got signed in, and sat kinda leaned onto one of those high seat chairs for old folks.

When I got called in and they asked to see my injuries, I tried rolling my pants up over my knee.

"Do you mind, doc, if I drop my pants to show you?"

Turns out my knee was the size of a cantaloupe . . . well . . . there was my knee, and then there was a cantaloupe-sized bulge on it.

Kinda of listing to the right.

My ankle? Barely swollen. Even a bit.

X-Ray. X-Ray. X-Ray. X-Ray. X-Ray.

Got put in a waiting area. Got a notification about test results. Got logged into MyChart. Knee? Massive tissue damage. Ankle? Non-displaced distal fibula fracture.

Found info on it at the Cleveland Clinic. (Sod off, Mayo!)

Now . . . if you have to hobble to your motorcycle on a PNW trail pick it up where it's fallen and ride it an hour to the ER? All on a broken ankle? A non-displaced distal fibula fracture is the ankle break to have . . . the fibula is basically non-weight bearing and non-displaced means it fractured in such a way as to need no surgical intervention.

Yay me.

Doc comes in. Tells me the results of the X-Rays. Puts me in a wheelchair. Rolls me to my room.

Gives me an ice pack. Gives me a pain med shot. Gives me a tetanus shot.

Leaves me alone for an hour.

Comes back. Looks at knee. Says she's worried about coagulated blood in the joint. Wants to aspirate. I said I don't know what that means, but she's the medical professional and have at it.

Stupid. Stupid. Stupid. Stupid.

It was the single most painful experience I've had my entire life . . . including having kidney stones so big they had to do that procedure where they blast them with sound waves to break them up . . . including sliding down the highway at, perhaps, extralegal speeds in sneakers, jeans, a t-shirt, and a motorcycle helmet after trying to do something extremely unwise . . . including that one time I asked Natalie Hassendorf out in the sixth grade with a note reading Do you want to go out with me? Check this box for Yes or this box for No during lunch, in the cafeteria, in front of all our classmates, and all the other grades - two through eight - at a catholic school . . . in full sight of nuns . . . and Father Daniel . . . and the principal.

You know they're the principal, not the principle, because they're your princiPAL.

The doc? She grabbed a syringe with a needle on it the size of a straw . . . a gosh darned straw! You know? Like those metal straws we were all supposed to buy a couple of years back to save the turtles?

I watched her stick it in.

Stupid.

I watched her root around inside my knee for just short of eternity about twenty seconds, all the time mumbling under her breath while grabbing the cantaloupe-sized part of my knee for leverage and squeezing.

Squeezing.

"Squeezing" is most assuredly not supposed to be onomatopoeical, but, I assure you, it can be under the proper circumstances.

After the twenty seconds was up she pulled the needle out and I asked if she'd gotten the blood. She replied that that had been the analgesic as she grabbed another syringe with another metal straw and poked a hole in me two inches away from where she had put the analgesic.

After a minute of the most intense pain I'd ever felt I asked if "analgesic" meant "numbing agent." She replied in the affirmative.

I asked if she were sure.

By this time I had interlaced my fingers and was squeezing my hands together so tightly that now, a week-and-a-half later, both my hands are still one big fading bruise.

Through gritted teeth I asked what she was saying (referring to her mumbling) and she replied that she couldn't find the knee joint.

Couldn't find the knee joint.

Now look, I understand not being able to find the knee joint when there's that much swelling and coagulated blood and soft tissue damage . . . truly . . . and I appreciate the effort by the doc to do what she thought was the right thing . . . but, after a minute of rooting around and getting nothing, she pulled the metal straw out of my swollen knee, grabbed another metal straw needle syringe thing, and said she was going to put in more analgesic.

Sometimes when you're a kid and you're playing secret agent and you get captured by the baddies and they start to "torture" you and you just smirk at them and say something like you'll never break me! or do your worst! cuz you know you're the toughest secret agent ever?

When I saw that third needle I started making a high-pitched whine and repeatedly said please oh please don't over and over again.

She did.

Then pulled it out, grabbed a fourth needle, and went to town again trying to find the knee joint.

I just couldn't anymore. I scrunched my eyes as tightly as I could. Squeezed my hands together as tightly as I could. Gritted my teeth together as grittily as I could.

I realized I would give her anything at all, tell her anything at all, and do anything at all for her if only she would stop.

Anything.

No secret agent am I.

She eventually stopped.

And left.

I have no idea, even now, a week-and-a-half later, if she aspirated my knee joint successfully.

Sometime later someone else came in to clean the open wounds on my knee - "debridement" they'd said while picking out gravel and other bits of Tillamook State Forest. Sometime later later someone else came in to put a splint on my ankle - they were happy I'd worn my motorcycle boots because it'd held my foot at the proper angle for them to not have to muscle my foot into place for the splint. Sometime later later later someone else came in and gave me some antibiotics and pain meds to take home. Sometime later later later later the doc came in to put a large adhesive bandage on my knee. Sometime later later later later later someone else came in to set me up with crutches and tell me a second doc was reviewing my X-Rays and then I could go.

Two-and-a-half hours later (later later later later later) no one had come in at all so, after convincing myself (after another twenty minutes) that it was okay to do so, I pushed the call button and someone else came in. I told them I wanted to go home. They looked me over. Said I looked good to go. Stepped out for a moment. Stepped back in. Told me to be careful on the crutches. Avoid putting any weight on the foot. Avoid stairs. Keep the leg elevated above my heart. Ice the knee.

I got up on the crutches. Avoided asking about the second doc's review of my X-Ray. Avoided looking at the bed where my leg hadn't been elevated above my heart for the past seven hours. Avoided looking at the single ice pack, covered in blood, that lie all squishy and warm on the bed, having melted five hours ago. Avoided the fact that in my two-story house there were no bathrooms on the first floor.

Eh, I thought as I made my way, slowly, out the ER doors and through the dark parking lot to my car, perhaps I should just observe today being National Let It Go Day.

09 July 2026

Getting old(er) has come up a lot lately.

You know . . . because I'm getting old(er).

And once you hit half a century old? It's all down hill.

If you're lucky.

Like, really lucky.

Those first five decades or so? Lots of hustle. You're trying to get a decent job, make enough money, get a decent place to live, navigate family and friend issues, live and love with romantic partners, fight the good fight for little personal things and big social issues, and make the world a gosh darn better place.

Oy vey!

If you're lucky . . . really lucky . . . at some point you've reached most of your personal goals (or are working towards them with a solid plan), have realized which goals are unlikely to be reached, and come to realize that your ability to make the world a better place is largely a (mostly) local thing.

Much of success in all this is simply luck. Knowing that luck could change, even in your 50s or later, is just something you 1) Work a bit toward mitigating the possibility of, and, 2) Accept that many of those things are just beyond your control.

You realize that it's not really your world anymore. There are decades of people born after you who are trying to hustle their way through life just like you did (again, if they're lucky) and they generally have better ideas and decades' less baggage to deal with. The best thing to do is let them go on being the (mostly) wonderful people they're going to be and try not to muck up their works too much.

Then there's death.

You realize, of course, that you're going to die. You can feel it coming . . . slowly . . . probably still decades away, but there . . . in the way that broken bones take a bit more healing and exercise gets a bit harder and memory starts to get a little less accurate.

So it really is all down hill. If you're over fifty and have been very lucky, you get to coast a bit. Oh sure you, you still have to put some effort into work and exercise and relationships and fighting the good fights, but you have those previous decades of experience to rely on to help you navigate those types of things.

You realize that you're not particularly relevant, and never have been, except, again, possibly on a hyper-local level . . . and, frankly, most of those people who strive to still be relevant on a larger scale? They're mostly just a bunch of (PDF), well, cunts.*

Don't be a cunt. Be relevant (in a good way!) to the people around you.

And enjoy, as much as you can, the process of getting old(er) - there's a lot of crap that comes with it, but there's some good stuff, too.
